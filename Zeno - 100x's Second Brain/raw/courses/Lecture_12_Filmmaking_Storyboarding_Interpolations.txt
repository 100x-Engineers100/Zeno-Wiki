SECTION 1 — CINEMATIC SHOT NODE (New Node Introduced)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

What it is: A specialized image generator node in FreePik
that gives you camera configuration controls instead of model selection.
Think of it as: your virtual camera crew.
Likely running Imagen 2 under the hood (based on output aesthetics).

Also accessible via: FreePik Home → "All Tools" → "Cinematic Shot" → pin it.

4 Camera Settings (this is the core of this section):

1. CAMERA (Film Stock)
   Controls color tone and dynamic range of the output.
   
   Options:
   - Standard: default, prompt-dependent output
   - Cinema Digital 35mm: modern Hollywood look, high dynamic range, rich colors
   - 70mm Film: grainy texture (Christopher Nolan aesthetic), warm grain
   - 35mm Film: classic cinematic look
   - 8mm Film: heavy grain, warm tone — retro/vintage movie feel
   - Camcorder: Y2K / 2000s aesthetic (Mr. Bean era look)
   - Medium Format Film: standard modern camera, clean, professional
   - Lo-Fi Toy Camera: high contrast, cheap plastic camera aesthetic,
     extreme color distortion (Lomo-style)

2. LENS (Optical Character)
   Controls how the lens filters light — adds optical "personality."
   
   Options:
   - Standard Lens: clean glass, no distortion, no character
   - Prime Lens: extremely sharp focus on subject (recommended by Pranay)
   - Anamorphic: oval bokeh (cinematic widescreen feel)
   - Classic Planar Prime: similar to prime, smooth bokeh, no distortion
   - Vintage Aspherical: soft edges, darker colors at edges — old TV look
   - Medium Format: good background separation, subject clear
   - Medium Format Smooth: soft focus roll-off in background

3. FOCAL LENGTH (Field of View / Perspective)
   Controls HOW MUCH of the world is in your frame.
   
   14–24mm (Ultra-Wide):
   - Massive establishing shots, grand environments
   - Low-angle establishing shots
   - Use for: "show me the whole world"
   
   35mm (Medium-Wide):
   - Subject grounded in environment
   - Both subject and background in detail
   - Closest to human peripheral vision

   50mm (Normal):
   - Mimics human eye
   - Zero distortion, realistic portrait
   - Background starts to blur slightly

   85–135mm (Telephoto):
   - Isolates subject, blurs background (bokeh)
   - Use for: close-up emotional shots, faces

   200mm+ (Extreme Tele):
   - Background is abstract/magnified and blurred out
   - Only subject in frame, background unrecognizable

   Rule: LOWER focal length = WIDER shot.
   HIGHER focal length = NARROWER, more isolated shot.

4. APERTURE (Depth of Field)
   Controls what is in focus and what is blurred.
   Think of it as: "how much of the scene is sharp."
   
   F1.2 (Ultra-Shallow):
   - Eyelashes sharp, everything else blurs immediately
   - Use for: extreme macro shots, eye close-ups
   
   F2–F2.8:
   - Subject in focus, background creamy blur
   - Classic portrait look
   
   F4–F5.6:
   - Subject sharp, background partially blurred
   - Standard "person + environment"
   
   F8:
   - Most things in focus
   - Good general use
   
   F11–F16:
   - Everything sharp, wide depth of field
   - Use for: wide landscape shots, everything must be clear

   Rule: LOWER aperture = SHALLOWER depth of field (more blur).
   HIGHER aperture = DEEPER depth of field (everything sharp).

Focal length vs. Aperture — the distinction:
- Focal length = field of view (how wide or narrow your frame is)
- Aperture = depth of field (how much of the frame is in focus)
Both can blur the background, but from different mechanisms.
Mechanical truth: focal length = how far the lens is from sensor.
Aperture = how open the iris of the lens is.

SECTION 2 — THE FILMMAKING PROCESS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

This is Pranay's actual process — start here every time you have to make a film.

PHASE 1 — NARRATIVE (Before touching any tool)
Compress your story into ONE LINE.
Example: "If a monster drinks Monster Energy drink, he becomes more destructive."
That one line = your creative north star. Every shot decision comes from it.

Storytelling frameworks (choose one or combine):
- Three-Act Structure: Setup → Confrontation → Resolution
- Hero's Journey: Ordinary world → Call to action → Transformation
- Save the Cat (Beat Sheet): Commercial storytelling method, hook-focused
- In Medias Res: Open in the middle of action (Christopher Nolan's method)
  Jump cut backwards and forwards in time. Attention-grabbing from frame 1.

PHASE 2 — CHARACTER & LOCATION SETUP
Before generating any scene, lock your characters and environments.
Why: if you change characters mid-way, consistency breaks.

For each character:
- Generate front-facing image
- Generate T-pose (captures full body anatomy, clothing, proportions)
- Generate side profile
- Generate contextual/emotional shots as needed

For each location:
- Generate a master establishing shot (wide, 14–24mm, F11)
- Generate alternative angles (low angle, eye level, drone)
- Keep location generation consistent — use the establishing shot as reference

PHASE 3 — STORYBOARD GENERATION
Each storyboard frame = one shot in your final film.
Build each frame as a separate image in FreePik Spaces.
Combine character references + location references in each shot image.
This IS your visual storyboard — AI-generated storyboard frames.

How to storyboard the Monster Energy example:
Shot 1 — Establishing: King Kong huge against city skyline, destroying buildings
Shot 2 — Mid-shot: King Kong sitting, looking tired among ruins
Shot 3 — Close-up: Man approaches, holds up energy drink (product)
Shot 4 — Close-up: King Kong's face as he reaches for drink
Shot 5 — Wide: King Kong drinks the can
Shot 6 — Wide: King Kong erupts with new energy, destruction escalates

PHASE 4 — GENERATING SCENE IMAGES
For each storyboard frame:
- Use a separate Image Generator node (or one per scene)
- Rename each node "Shot 1", "Shot 2" etc. for clarity
- Connect relevant character references + location references
- Set aspect ratio: 16:9 for landscape video
- Match camera settings to the shot mood:
  Establishing → wide focal (14–24mm), F11
  Close-up face → tight focal (85–135mm), F2.8
  Dramatic hero moment → anamorphic lens, 50mm

PHASE 5 — VIDEO GENERATION (Interpolation)
Turn storyboard images into video clips.

Two core video strategies:

STRATEGY 1 — DIRECT PROMPTING
Feed a single image as the start frame.
Write a motion prompt: "King Kong reaches for the drink, hand extending slowly"
Model: Kling 3.0 or CDance 2.0

STRATEGY 2 — INTERPOLATION (Start + End Frame)
Feed Shot 1 image as START and Shot 2 image as END.
Model creates the transition motion between the two.
Use this when: you need a character to move from one specific pose to another.
Use direct prompting when: you want more creative freedom in the motion.

Note from Pranay: When using interpolation with references,
realism can decrease. For photorealistic outputs, use direct prompting.
For controlled transitions, use interpolation.

PHASE 6 — COMBINING VIDEO CLIPS
Use the VIDEO COMBINER node in FreePik Spaces.
Connects multiple video outputs in sequence.
Set sequence order at the bottom of the node.
Final output: your assembled ad film.

For transitions between clips (fade, cut, zoom):
Do NOT try to handle transitions in AI prompts — results are inconsistent.
Use traditional editing software: CapCut, Premiere Pro, Final Cut Pro.
CapCut can even add transitions, captions (auto-captions via AI).

SECTION 4 — PROMPTING GUIDE (Key Principles)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

For image prompts in a film workflow:
- Always specify: subject + action + environment + camera angle + mood
- Reference ALL key assets via connected nodes + @ syntax
- Say "THE camera" not "a camera" for UGC-style facing shots
- Use cinematic vocabulary: "low angle", "drone shot", "establishing shot",
  "close-up", "rack focus", "bokeh"
